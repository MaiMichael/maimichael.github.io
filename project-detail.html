Hollow Descent

A psychological horror game where the environment warps in response to player decisions. Every room remembers what you did — and punishes you for it.

Engine Unreal Engine 5
Genre Psychological Horror
Team 10 people
Duration 14 months
Platform PC / Console
POV First Person

Design Intent

The core question: What if a horror game's environment was an unreliable narrator? Most horror games use scripted scares tied to fixed locations. We wanted the space itself to become adversarial — changing layout, lighting, and object placement based on what the player had already done.

The design goal was to make players distrust their own memory of the space. If you backtracked through a room you'd already cleared, it might not be the same room anymore. This created a persistent low-level dread that scripted scares alone can't achieve.

Role & Responsibilities

As Lead Designer, I owned the core experience from pitch through vertical slice and into production. My responsibilities spanned design, production, and narrative integration:

Designed the core loop: explore → trigger environmental shift → re-navigate altered space → encounter escalation
Authored and maintained a 40-page design document covering systems, pacing, and narrative beats
Managed sprint planning and daily standups across design, art, audio, and engineering
Ran weekly playtests, synthesized feedback, and prioritized iteration backlog
Collaborated with the writer to integrate narrative reveals into environmental shifts
Process

Development moved through three major phases. Early work focused on nailing the feeling of spatial unreliability before we invested in content. Here's how the design evolved:

🖼️
Early blockout — The initial graybox layout exploring sightlines and room connectivity. We tested 4 layout variants before landing on the hub-and-spoke structure.
🖼️
Shift system v1 — First pass at environmental shifts. Rooms would swap props and lighting on re-entry.
🖼️
Shift system v3 — Final version: layout itself changes. Doorways reposition, corridors extend, dead-ends appear.
🖼️
Pacing diagram — Tension curve mapped against player progression. Peaks aligned with shift-trigger moments.
🖼️
Final art pass — The main hall after full environment art, lighting, and post-processing.

The biggest design pivot came at month 6. Playtesters weren't noticing subtle shifts — we had to make the environment changes feel wrong rather than just look different. This led to audio design becoming a core pillar: spatial audio cues that signal something has changed before the player sees it.

Takeaways

Every project teaches you something. Here's what I carried forward from Hollow Descent:

What worked

Playtesting early and often. We ran 12 external playtests before vertical slice, which caught the "players don't notice subtle changes" problem early enough to pivot.

What worked

Giving audio equal weight to visuals in the design doc. The spatial audio cues became the project's signature — testers consistently cited sound as the scariest element.

What I'd change

Scope ambition earlier. We designed 12 unique shift states per room but shipped 5. Better to have scoped to 6 from the start and polished all of them.

What I'd change

More structured handoffs between design and environment art. Some rooms went to art before the shift system was finalized, creating rework.
